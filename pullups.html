How to correct pullups

Hi, my name is Saurav and I'm a personal trainer. Today I'm going to show you how to do a pull-up correctly. Pull-ups are a great exercise for building upper body strength, and they're also a great way to improve your grip strength.

Benefits of pull-ups:

Pull-ups are a compound exercise, which means they work multiple muscle groups at the same time. The main muscle groups involved in pull-ups are the latissimus dorsi (lats), the trapezius (traps), the biceps brachii, and the rhomboids. Pull-ups also help to improve grip strength, core strength, and shoulder stability.

How to do a pull-up:

1. Find a pull-up bar that is at about chest height.


2. Hang from the bar with your hands shoulder-width apart, and your palms facing away from you.


3. Engage your core and pull yourself up until your chin is over the bar.

4. Slowly lower yourself back down to the starting position.

5. Repeat for the desired number of repetitions.

Tips for doing pull-ups:

* Keep your back straight throughout the exercise.
* Don't let your elbows flare out to the sides.
* Pull yourself up using your back muscles, not your arms.
* If you can't do a full pull-up, you can start by doing assisted pull-ups. To do this, have a friend or partner help you by lifting your body up until your chin is over the bar.

Common mistakes to avoid: * Not engaging your core. * Letting your back round. * Flaring your elbows out to the sides. * Using your arms to pull yourself up. * **How to progress with pull-ups:** As you get stronger, you can increase the number of pull-ups you do in a set, or you can try doing pull-ups with a weight belt. You can also try different variations of pull-ups, such as wide-grip pull-ups, chin-ups, or muscle-ups.

Safety: If you have any pain in your shoulders or back, stop doing pull-ups and consult with a doctor or physical therapist. I hope this helps!

Additional tips: * If you're struggling to do a full pull-up, you can try doing negatives. To do this, start at the top of the pull-up position and slowly lower yourself down to the bottom. * You can also try doing banded pull-ups. To do this, wrap a resistance band around the pull-up bar and place it under your feet. This will help to provide you with some assistance. * As you get stronger, you can gradually reduce the assistance provided by the band or the number of negatives you do.

Progression chart: The following chart shows a suggested progression for pull-ups. As you get stronger, you can increase the number of sets and repetitions you do.

Week 1: * 3 sets of 3 pull-ups

Week 2: * 3 sets of 5 pull-ups

Week 3: * 3 sets of 8 pull-ups

Week 4: * 3 sets of 10 pull-ups

Week 5: * 3 sets of 12 pull-ups

Week 6: * 3 sets of 15 pull-ups

Week 7: * 3 sets of 20 pull-ups

Week 8: 3 sets of as many pull-ups as possible

Troubleshooting: If you're not able to do any pull-ups, don't worry! It takes time and practice to build up the strength to do a pull-up. Start by doing negatives or banded pull-ups, and gradually work your way up to doing full pull-ups. If you're experiencing pain in your shoulders or back, stop doing pull-ups and consult with a doctor or physical therapist.
